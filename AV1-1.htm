The honeyhangs are almost at full-bloom! Mister Martin told me once that a teaspoon of powder made from the petals can make any drink taste heavenly sweet. Oh, if only I could have them now.

Alright! I turn away from the window and count the cups I have already prepared. Miss Alice likes hers as dense as appropriate; Miss Violet prefers hers to have more moonlaces than redseeds; Mister Martin wants it hot on every day of the week but Tasday, which is not today; and Miss Emilia always likes it cold, with icefoam filling up to the brim of the mug. Four cups down, one more to go: a very specially-made one for Grandmama.

I have thought long and hard last night about this last cup. Grandmama has always had the members of the household take turns giving her their ‘brew of the day’, so I am not certain of her preferences. But I will put all my heart into it! The heart should be sweeter than honeyhangs, I hope! Though…it cannot be too sweet, of course.

Humming along to the familiar melody that the piano is singing, I take a cup from the rack, the dark red one that Grandmama always uses, the red as deep as the red of the bricks that make this house. I turn on the water dispenser, adjusting it to as hot as hot can be, for it is said that hot drinks are good for everyone, and I fill half the cup. Then into it I add: a finger-long moonlace, seven redseeds, five spoonsful of blackapple syrup, two pinches of crushed wakingleaves, and finally a whole spoonful of winddust. Stirring the mixture, I count to a hundred, and then it is done.

The whole kitchen is filled with the scent of blackapple, with a slight hint of moonlace and redseed. I wonder if I should add another spoonful of winddust, or maybe half a spoonful, just for Grandmama’s cup, to make the tastes of the ingredients melt more into one another. Putting more of it does not change the result in any way other than making everything feel smoother, but winddust is expensive. No, then.

I hope the smell does not reach outside the kitchen. I want this to be a surprise for everyone, though I also worry if they would prefer to have made the drinks themselves. I know that Grandmama would accept it in earnest no matter what, but…Well, I tried my best. Let me hope my best is enough. It is the heart that matters, is it not?

One by one, carefully, I put the five cups on a tray, and carefully lift it up to carefully carry it to the living room, where through a window I see Mister Martin bending over a flowerbed, probably propping the stems. I will use the second-floor external staircase to get to him later. Miss Violet is still playing the piano, a sweet morning tune, and so I decide not to bother her yet. For now, I head up the main stairway and then down the right corridor to Grandmama’s room. I open the door.

“Good morning!”

Grandmama turns and smiles to me through the glass sliding door, putting her book down on her lap. I make sure not to pull on the door too forcefully as I enter the balcony, and the rising morning instantly splashes onto my face from the side.

“Grandmama! I made this for you.” I look down onto my tray, counting to the third cup from the left on the second row. “Here. Your daily blackapple tea, Aesena’s brew!”

Grandmama’s golden eyes lit up, like the twinkles of stars, and her whitening black hair a twinkling night sky. “Oh, you good girl. Thank you, Aesy.” She receives the cup happily. “What ma-”

“Excuse me, Grandmama, but I’ll come back later. Four more cups to deliver!" I lift the tray back up and hurry – though in a careful not-so-hurried fashion – down the steps at the side of the balcony.

As I walk to Mister Martin, who is now tending the waterstalks, rows of sunflowers face up and bloom in the direction of the sun, the sunrays waking them up from their slumber, and never before have I felt so lucky for the coincidence. It is like they are telling me that the day will be different. And how will it not? I am so off to such a good start.

He is wearing a dark blue flatcap so as to give his eyes a shade from the sunlight, to let him focus on his work more conveniently.

“Good morning, Mister Martin. I made blackapple tea for you. It’s still hot.” I lift up the lid to let the steam prove my point.

He turns away from the stalks, cautiously making sure his release does not leave them swinging. “Why thank you, young madam Aesena. A very good morning to you too.” He accepts it! He lifts the cup from the tray and takes a sniff and then a sip, his smile widening. “Thank you. This is good, just the way I like it. The wakingleaves are just right. But how did you know?” He looks a little surprised.

“You told me once a few days ago. I remember. I have to go give Miss Violet her cup now. I hope you enjoy it!” I wave as I backstep.

This is turning out really well. Ah! Miss Alice is dusting the bell, preparing the ring of the morning. The bell’s usual shining red is painted over by the deep orange that is so unfurling, though it is turning into a brighter yellow now that the sun is rising higher. I change my course towards the tower.

“Miss Alice!” I call, and she leans out to look down with her hand holding onto a pillar. “I made tea! Your morning tea!”

I hear an unclear ‘Hold on!’ a brief second before the bell rings, and then in another minute I hear footsteps coming downwards.

“Young Aesena, what have you there?” She comes out into the clearing, her dark blonde hair bouncing, her white dress flowing in the wind of her hurry.

“Blackapple.” I show her, handing over her cup. “Yours has eight spoonsful of the syrup, with nine redseeds. Did I get it right?”

Miss Alice gasps. “You’re wonderful! Thank you. What brought this up?”

I tilt my head in contemplation of her question, then I understand. “Just…wanted to.” I give her a sheepish smile before looking down at my tray. “I still have…Do you know where Miss Emilia is? Or Miss Violet?”

Miss Alice takes a gulp of her tea then points to the aviary tower. “Miss Emilia was heading there when I passed her just now.”

“Thank you.” I adjust my grip on the tray and stride off.

The red-stained glass that makes the dome of the aviary has not yet ceased to impress me. The entrance is a tower similar to the others, however, though brick and glass somewhat fit aesthetically. I knock twice on the door, clearly, before going in and through the small archway. The chirps of birds louden, those of the brightwings more distinctive to my ears.

Branches and perches and hang-it-downs fill the dome to fullness, and the rays of sunlight are brighter than they are outside. Here and there are bushes of redseeds and dandelnuts grown by the plentiful. “Are you in here, Miss Emilia?” I keep my voice soft as to not startle the birds. A triplet of doves sweeps over my head, and I bend by instinct. “Miss Emilia?” I try to spot that gleaming pair of green eyes that even all the green in this place cannot hide, then I see her at the section of the white branches. “Miss Emilia, good morning. I made tea.”

Miss Emilia lifts her head up. “Oh, good morning, young miss. Pardon me, but come again?” She strokes the wings of an owlet as she comes, the bird sleepily perched on her wrist.

I put the tray on a log and picks up Miss Emilia’s cup. “I made your morning tea. Cold as you prefer it.”

“Ah, so you did. I was just wanting to go to the kitchens. Thank you very much.” She takes a long sip, holding the cup with one hand, and not by the handle. “And I observed that you used ‘Good morning’ rather than ‘Well dawn’, but you need not make this particular adjustment, you know?”

I pick the tray back up. “Just going for something different.”

“You’re a bright lady.” She looks down. “Who is this last cup for? Your grandmother? She told me you wish to start your classes in two days.”

Ah. I nod. “Yes, that’s right. I’m looking forward to Anglish Literature and World History. You’ll be covering them, right, Miss Emilia? Can we do History first?”

She laughs. “Of course. It gladdens me to do so.”

“Oh, this is for Miss Violet,” I answer, returning to her question. “Everyone else already has theirs. So to the piano I go.”

Miss Emilia ponders. “I think she said she’s going into town. She told me so yesterday.” She then puts a finger to her lips, listening intently. “Her playing has stopped, which means she’s probably about to make her own cup of tea by now. You must go immediately if you want to catch her.”

“Oh no! Then I must leave. Thank you, Miss Emilia!” I turn around quickly.

“Thank you again, young miss, this is a very pleasant surprise.” I hear her say, and I send a speedy wave to the back before going out the door.

I wonder if Miss Violet can take two cups of tea, but I best make it in time. The redness of the dome got my vision whirling now that my eyes are adjusting back to the outside light. I push open the sidedoor into the house with my elbow and brisk up my pace to the kitchen.

But it is empty, and in a fumbled confusion I turn around and almost crashed into Miss Violet.

“Miss Violet! Sorry!” I get out of her way at once, but then she clasps both my shoulders with her hands.

“Steady, my lady.” And I compose myself.

I quickly turn around to place the tray on the table and grab the handle of the last cup on it, extending it to Miss Violet. “I made this for you.” I made it in time! I lift the lid.

The aroma of blackapples still lingers in the kitchen. Miss Violet takes in a deep breath with the brim of the cup under her nose, her one hand on the handle and the other supporting the side with the tips of her fingers. I wonder if it is still as hot as I made it to be. I wait, and then she drinks.

“Thank you, my lady. You’re too sweet.” Her bright blue eyes ripple, her golden hair complementing them, and I cannot help being mesmerized, but after a while I manage to look away shyly.

I sigh with relief, the mission of the morning accomplished.

Miss Violet lifts her cup to drink again. “Is there anything I can do for you?” She asks after.

I play with the locks behind my ear. “I’ll ready my laundry later…and um, can I go to town with you today?”

She widens her eyes. “Oh?” Then she smiles. “Best next time, my lady.”

“Perhaps during our first Art lesson?” I press on.

Miss Violet looks at me for a long moment before nodding. “That sounds nice. Till then then.” She has such a pretty smile. “Thank you for your blackapple tea, Lady Aesena. It has made my morning.”

I beam with pride.

She continues: “Lady Alserway asked for you just now. Goodbye for the moment.” She steps aside.

Grandmama? Oh yeah.

“Goodbye Miss Violet. Be careful on your way.” I curtsy.

I walk out the kitchen and into the main hallway then up the stairway, turning right to the corridor and into Grandmama’s room again.

“How do you find it, Grandmama?” I go in front of her, her book still on her lap, opened but probably the same page. She was looking in the direction of the sun with the cup in her hands, and now she turns to face me.

“Aesy, sit, please.” She gestures to a resting cushion beside her.

So I sit, looking at Grandmama eagerly. She sips, the cup still steaming from the inside.

She lowers the cup, and looks out from the balcony again. “This is the warmest morning I ever had. It also looks like you’ve given everyone such a morning today.”

I bounce excitedly on the cushion before swinging my legs. “Happy to be of service!” I softly clap my hands thrice, then lay my cheek on the back of one of them.

“You look notably adventurous today. What do you have planned, my dear?” Grandmama remarks.

I stop my leg-swinging to muse on the question. “Um, nothing, I guess. Just seeing where this will take me.” I continue swinging.

“What a wonderful sentiment,” she cheers, “and my thoughts exactly. Why don’t we have Alice prepare a picnic basket for you?”

I stop swinging again. “But I have gone for a picnic twice already. There’s nothing much to see after the first time,” I pause, “But I would if you come too.” I fill my eyes with as much hope as I can.

Grandmama laughs. “My legs do not fancy adventure, Aesy. Listen.” She shifts on her chair, leaning over to me. “The first time and the times after are never the same, as long as you know how they can vary. There’s always something new for you to stumble upon, if you just make yourself ready for it, with an open eye.”

That is what I want. I cannot waste such a good start of the day. It must continue to be as filled with activities as possible.

I stand up. “Okay. I’ll ask Miss Alice. Can I ask her to come too, if she isn’t busy?”

“She most probably is, but you can try. You might be lucky,” Grandmama smiles, “Have a great day. Look for something different. But don’t go too far.”

I nod. “Yes, Grandmama. I’ll tell you all about it later. Have a great day too!” And off I go, wondering if indeed there would be anything to tell about.

Miss Alice must probably be at her office now. I think her chore hours are thirty to thirty-six in the afternoon, which makes it more likely for me to successfully get her to accompany me.

The frontyard of the observatory tower is specifically for petalballs and ladyhats, Mister Martin said, where elsewhere is more of a mix of flower species. There is another similar section at the near back of the compound used for gemlilies, the main source of income for the manor.

I bend down to regard one of the shrubs. I still think that the person who gave the name ‘ladyhat’ was making a joke. The flowers do not look like hats at all, much less something a lady would wear. I straighten back up and head to the stairs lining the external walls of the tower, the red railing casting long tendril-like shadows on the red bricks.

The door is unlocked. I was right.

“Excuse me, Miss Alice? It’s me again,” I announce.

Miss Alice looks up from the sheets of paper in front of her. “Young Aesena? Do you have another cup for me?”

The question surprises me. “Hm? I can go-”

She giggles. “I was kidding. Come in.” Miss Alice is ever in a cheery mood. Her desks are filled with stacked-high books and documents, with only one of them having a writing space that even so is hardly big enough. The other members of the household have many books too in their respective rooms, also as stacked as Miss Alice’s.

Two fifths of the glass dome roof are slid open while the others are covered by metal panels. Miss Alice once showed me the starry sky when she opened all five of the panels. That was…the second night after I reached, I think.

“Your blackapple tea was as wonderful as you, young Aesena. Thank you again.” Miss Alice rolls up the papers.

Joy fills my heart once more. “I’m very happy to hear that.”

She turns to me, the rolls of paper in both her hands. “Is there anything you need of me?”

“Grandmama told me to go out on a picnic again. Can you help me?”

“Yes, immediately. Let’s go.” She puts down the papers before walking to me at the door.

“Can you come with me too?” I ask, a little meekly.

Miss Alice stops in her steps. “You’re very lovely, young Aesena, but I must take a rain check on that. I need to rush on a project.” Her expression turns apologetic.

“Oh. That’s okay.” I try my best to hide my disappointment, but I really do not know how. “What are you working on?” I move the conversation elsewhere.

“It’s almost mid-Oscy, and this year the Butterfly Comet is coming again.” Miss Alice must have picked up the hint on my attempt. “Do you know of the Butterfly Comet?” She walks me out, closing the door to the observatory and letting me go down the stairs first.

I nod. I remember the children’s story.

“They also say that the left wing of the Butterfly Constellation might be seen at this part of the world,” she continues, “I wish to chart the factors of how that can happen. We might even see the comet pass in front of the wing, even though we’re nowhere near Choushi. The wing might overlap with the Dancing Crab, however, and the Winged Arrow is acting really weird this year.”

She lost me at ‘Choushi’.

“You didn’t get that last part, did you?” Miss Alice notices, giggling. “I can’t wait to teach you about the skies. You’re in for a huge treat, young Aesena.” She spins once and hops over a shrub of petalballs.

I am excited to learn more in-depth about the suns and the moons and the stars, though I think it will be overwhelming for my brain. And I am honestly more interested in the other subject that Miss Alice will be covering: Magical Science.

We reach the kitchen. Miss Alice gets to work by making toasts before starting up one of the stoves for a quick cheese omelette. Then in the oven she heats up clouds and bacon and sausages before frying them. I find it hard to keep up with her swift and naturally precise movements such that my ‘assistance’ might just be getting in her way.

Before long, everything is readied and packed in a basket, with the utensils and plates plus a blanket and an umbrella.

“I really feel bad about not joining you. Can you ask me again next time?” Miss Alice hands the basket to me.

I nod fervently. “Definitely, Miss Alice, I understand. Thank you so much for your help.”

“Off you go now. The morning is still young, with plenty of sunshine to bathe in, plenty of wind to dance with, plenty of colours to sing of, plenty of chances to embrace, and plenty of others and plenty of more. Make the best of it.” She ushers me out the frontdoor, and waves to me as I turn to go.

I skip to the gates before turning back one more time to give Miss Alice a big wave. Then I continue skipping, the basket swinging lightly.
