1 - 9

Tomorrow is the day. Tomorrow is the day. I keep repeating it as I wait for sleep to take me, but it does not. My heart is pounding like drums at a festival, my blood flowing rapid and hot like a river of liquid fire. The blanket feels like a bother at times, and at other moments it is as if the bedsheet itself is made out of needles. Tomorrow is the day.

But wait. I squint at the window, the curtains gently rippling. The sun is up already! A brightening dawn, with the red just beginning to fade into an orange, but the morning nonetheless. I sit up agitatedly, rocking the bed.

Today is the day! If my soul could leak out onto the floor it very surely would. I have missed the opportunity to savour the comfort of my feathery bed for the last time before what is to come, and have instead squandered it away because of my restlessness. I wonder if I have actually slept at all, and have not merely been trying to the entire night.

Never mind that. It will be a week before I can take a proper shower again. If I do not take one now, I will be sure to regret it.

Then as I stumble into a knee-length dress, I hear the sound of the piano. The manor has woken up. And that gives me an idea: to start the day like I started that one day when everything started; a fitting routine. I head down to the kitchens.

I ready six mugs, one of them Grandmama’s dark red one, and gather up the ingredients on a tray. My hands are quicker now, I consciously notice, as one hand swipes redseeds and moonlaces off the tray to add into the brew while the other stirs. All that practice from potion-making, no doubt.

Potions…did I properly prepare those that I might need? I will have to check later.

I focus on the moment’s task: eight spoonsful of blackapple syrup and nice redseeds for Miss Alice, icefoam and four spoonsful of syrup for Miss Emilia, two fingers of moonlaces for Miss Violet, and since today is not Tasday Mister Martin’s should be hot with three crunches of wakingleaves.

“Ellamisceo,” I whisper, and tiny whirlpools are formed in all four cups, stirring the mixture Magically, and as they do, I carefully pour winddust into each of them.

I ponder on Grandmama’s cup. How did I do it last time? Hm…today’s mood then.

A finger-long moonlace, six redseeds, six spoonsful of the syrup, two pinches of wakingleaves…That should be perfect! I will use the same measurement for my own cup, like how I usually asked for. “Ellamisceo,” I call again after adding hot water to both Grandmama’s cup and mine, slowly tipping the container of winddust towards them one by one, watching as every sprinkle melts instantly. It gives off an extremely satisfying sensation.

I look out the window to let the morning into my eyes, keeping my sight on the gleaming dewdrops on a glazetilly shrub for a long moment, reluctant to turn away, wanting to continue gazing blankly, but I eventually return to my work.

Finally, with all the cups filled and neatly arrayed on the tray, I add the one ingredient I did not have the chance to use the three times before today: honeyhangs. Two petals each except for Miss Alice’s and Mister Martin’s, both who would prefer the bitterness of their tea to remain strong, so I only put a single petal onto theirs respectively.

I wondered if I should have used the grinded powder of the honeyhang petals instead, but that would have been too sweet for a waking morning. I pick up the tray and exit the kitchen.

Miss Alice stops just in time as the both of us round the same corner. “Whoopsie-lasiely,” she exclaims a little too loudly, then winces as she remembers the piano playing in the background, and we giggle softly. “Good morning. I was just about to come ready your picnic basket for you. It is a bit earlier today because I have some errands to run in town with Miss Violet.” Then she notices the tray in my hands.

“Good morning. The…” I count, “middle one of the row nearest to you, that one’s yours. Do you usually use honeyhangs for your blackapple tea?” I want to make sure.

“That’s alright. I enjoy anything that you make, young Aesena,” she rubs my head, “Thank you so much.” She picks her cup up and takes a sip, swooning adorably. “What a start of a lovely day. Well, let me get to work. Come back in fifteen minutes, okay?”

“Okay.” ‘A start of a lovely day’…My head spins for a while before I continue walking, then up the stairs to the second floor. On my turning into the right corridor, I look behind to the music room, all the way to the end of the manor’s left wing, and I see the silhouettes of both Mister Martin and Miss Emilia leaning onto the side of the piano as it plays. I cannot spot Miss Violet from where I stand. I change directions, starting down the left corridor instead.

I stop at the doorway to quietly announce my presence before stepping in. But it does not seem necessary for me to retain my careful silence. Mister Martin and Miss Emilia are chatting in a casual volume, and Miss Violet with her graceful and composed demeanour does not seem to mind them as her fingers dance across the piano keys.

A feeling engulfs me: how during my mission I will miss her music lessons so, so much. I shake it off.

“Good morning, young miss. Up so early again.” The room has a long column of windows, some opened and some not, and in the glare of the sunlight they allow in, Miss Emilia’s eyes sparkle a pure emerald green.

Miss Violet looks up briefly from her piano to acknowledge me, her stunning seablue eyes capturing me entirely in their hold, if only for a fleeting moment. Then she nods and returns her focus to her fingers and the keys and the springily upbeat melody.

I show them the tray. Mister Martin beams with a charming smile that befits his gentle smiling eyes and faint facial hair that smilingly frames his face. “Lucky day,” he announces. The windows in this room makes me notice everyone’s eyes more, for some reason.

“Indeed.” Miss Emilia comes over to lift the tray from my hands and places them on a high table beside an ornamental vase.

So now my hands are free to serve their respective cups to them. Miss Violet stops the piece prematurely, but with enough grace that it somehow sounds completed, and stands up from the piano, coming over as well.

“Wonderful as always, you and your brew, my lady,” she says after a sip.

I curtsy to my tutors. “Happy to be of service.”

I pick the tray back up, waving my goodbyes and trading best wishes for the day, to continue on towards Grandmama’s room. I open the door with my elbow.

“Good morning, Grandmama.” I relieve myself of the tray then extend the cup to Grandmama.

As always, she is reading a book, this one titled ‘The Fifty-Night Roving’. She wedges a bookmark in between the pages before closing it. “Aesy.” Grandmama calls. “Ah, another special day has arrived.” She receives the cup.

I take a seat down beside her, bringing my own cup to my face, letting the vapour thinly plume upwards onto my face. I take in the smell of the blackapple tea, redseeds and moonlaces and all. Then I drink, slowly because it is very hot. I sigh softly, satisfied for the while.

Grandmama turns to me, her head leaned back. “The plan for the day?” I know it is a question because we do this on a daily basis.

I smile. “Adventure.” That is how I call my ‘picnics’, because they are not mere picnics. And today will be far from being one. Today the word ‘adventure’ rings true, like bells, bells that are sounding louder as each second passes.

“This book I’m reading is just about that. It’s quite the journey actually,” she raises the book to show me the cover, “A sailor had to leave the sea for a forest then a desert, both terrains that he was not suited to, to find and deliver items. I’m at the part where he needed to return to his ship, and from there sail to a remote island, then the path would still take him further outwards into uncharted waters. All in fifty days.” She puts the book back down onto her lap.

“You’ve read it before?” I lean against an armrest so that I am tilting in my seat in her direction.

“Yes. Of course, he would reach home in the end, sit down by a warm fire with a hot cup of something in his hand, and put down all his doings-and-goings on paper. I think such adventures can be endlessly reread, as a sort of reflection.” Grandmama is such a romantic. “I imagine that I am that sailor, reading the words that I’ve myself written, vividly – but this time safely of course – experiencing all that again. It’s a heavenly feeling that really fills me up.”

I cannot help but widen my smile at that. I think of how, at the end of my adventure, I will surely come home no matter what. I will be fine.

At least my adventure is not the one lasting fifty days, right?

“That’s a lovely book review,” I remark, “I’ll have to borrow it for a read when you’re done.” Did I say the same thing yesterday morning? That book was titled ‘By The Sandy Grey Beaches’, if I remember correctly, a thinner novel.

“Of course. By the end of today, you will.” Her golden eyes sparkle.

I drink, then I stand. “Well, Miss Alice must be done by now. I’ll get going.” I give Grandmama a hug and then a kiss on both cheeks.

“Have a great time, Aesy.”

“You too, Grandmama.”

And back to the kitchens I go, where Miss Alice hands me my basket, the whole of it filled with bacon-and-egg wraps, chicken sandwiches, clouds and sausages and slices of carrot cake. There is also a small bottle of fruit milk. She walks me to the gate then bids me a good day, and I return the wish, thanking her again for the food.

“See if you can find any stars on the ground today, young Aesena!” She calls loudly when I am halfway up the hill, and we share a laugh between our distance.

Then halfway downhill, I double back. I look at the manor one more time, almost like it is a dramatic final gaze. It is not, of course, I will return soon. Miss Alice has already gone back inside. I look at the red towers and the red mansion, the chimneys and the bell.

“Goodbye, towers. Goodbye, mansion. Goodbye, chimneys. Goodbye, bell.” After a while, I resume my walk.

And soon I am in the library, leaning onto the back-supporter of the big red chair while I munch on a chicken sandwich, surveying the things that I have prepared. I try to keep myself from feeling too tensed, because that would surely make Miss Breezy tensed herself. Of course, she is doing the same with me in consideration. Tenseness is contagious.

Self-knitted baggy-and-raggy dresses are laid folded and stacked, various potions in small spherical vials glint from the lights in the ceiling. Other than that, there are: a bunch of maps and another bunch of noteslips, a silver dagger from the training field, a ten-metre rope and a feathered sleepmat. I did find some Conjuration scrolls that would give me some kind of meal if I ever need to open them, and I am planning to bring three, just in case.

My Magic Bag, also self-knitted, sits in the centre of it all, waiting to swallow everything up. Floating by my side is my cloak, a dark crimson, also self-knitted. With Magic, of course, and under Miss Breezy’s supervision.

Miss Breezy told me that it is best not to bring any suspiciously-modern items over to the faraway past, for obvious reasons. No phones, no torchlights, no firelighters, no canned food, no modern-style clothes, no sleeping bags and no ballpens and the many like. I will be in for an authentic fifth-century experience. Woohoo.

“All set, I guess.” I stretch my hands out.

“Have you stored your mags? There won’t be an abundance of it there like here in the study.” Miss Breezy is holding back her voice. She wants to ensure I have everything prepared without filling the question with…What word is most suitable to define it? ‘Excitement’? ‘Worry’? ‘Anxiety’? ‘Anxiety’.

“Yup. I just need to release them when I get there.”

“Are you sure about your garments? How about u-”

“Stacked and packed,” I point, then I point at what I am wearing.

“Did you take that list of local herbs? You can gather them to brew-”

“Yes, it’s all here. You already reminded me about that, Miss Breezy,” I smile. “I’ll be fine. I’m destined to be fine. The journal says it’ll be easy.” I do not think it is necessary to bring along the journal, but I did make a copy of the first and second entry.

“Maybe there is a reminder that I must make in order for you to be fine. I’m destined to be worried.”

“Sorry about that,” I giggle, “But we did our best. Our very best. This month was awesome!” I announce for the whole library to hear, all the books and bottles and orbs of light, and maybe the training field and the log-and-rock can hear me too.

I truly believe that it was the best month of my life: Valvor of the year 2004. The month of a totally new and lifechanging beginning. The month of lessons with established tutors no one could get anywhere else in the known world. The month of adventure. Adventure!

Something is stinging my heart. It is beating too quickly. Or too slowly. I do not know.

“Anyway, how will this work?” How curious. After all that we have discussed, the topic of how the time-jumping works has not yet been breached.

“When you’re ready, when you feel you are, imagine a large mirror in an oval frame of gold and silver.” Miss Breezy answers. “You do not need to know the details of how this particular Conjuration works. Just think about it and it will come. It cannot help but come.”

I turn to my stuff. “Saccusin.” And everything takes turns hopping into the Magic Bag. When it is the potions’ turn, I make sure to keep a count: five Second Winds, five Higher Drives, five Senses Surges, three Senses Dimmings, six Quick Minds, one Invisibility Potion and a few others.

I head to the alchemy wing and take another Invisibility Potion. One more will not hurt. I should be careful about using it though; its ingredients are listed as rare. I throw the vial into the bag and it is chucked down with the other contents. “Yippa,” I call, and the bag leaps into my arms, and I sling it over me diagonally, like strapping on a pouch.

A mirror, huh?

Do I have any questions left that I need answers to right now, right before everything?

“Am I ready?” I ask Miss Breezy, and I ask myself.

“More ready than you’ll ever be,” she quips, and I notice the slight word change in the common phrase.

Clearing my mind and picturing a mirror, I wave my hands at where my stuff was a minute ago, and there before me springing-from-nowhere is an oval mirror twice my height, rimmed with gold and silver. If I look closely, the glass swirls ever so slightly, and on it my reflection ripples.

“Holy golly woah.” I step backwards, the desk behind me supporting one of my hands as the other clings onto the bag.

Staring at it now in its reflective splendour, I wonder if my present knowledge might prove sufficient. The things I learnt about time-travelling do not add up to much, since there is not a single book about it in the library, which is very off-putting, but Miss Breezy told me that there should be a reason to it.

It is probably like my destiny telling me not to worry and just go with what I know, only letting me learn everything that is necessary to progress and then to learn even more sooner or later. So I am not worrying. Or am I?

“The gate will directly bring you to where you need to be,” Miss Breezy tells me, her voice above me, I think.

A thought speeds through my head. “You’ve seen this before. It is the same kind of Timegate, right?” I ask her for reassurance, facing upwards.

“Yes, just the way I remember it. It’s only been a while since I first saw it, so it’s still a fresh memory.”

“I have another question, a funny one.”

“Ask away.”

I giggle. “By any chance, could you be the future me, painting yourself invisible and flying around holding a microphone?”

“That would be such a nice twist, wouldn’t it?” Miss Breezy laughs, an angelic sound. “Good one, but…,” she laughs again, “But no, I’m just a voice.”

The joke did its job. The atmosphere feels much lighter now. ‘A joke, well-placed, no matter the situation, could clear up any fog of thoughts’, I read that saying somewhere.

“Thank you, Miss Breezy, for all that we’ve been through together this past month. Please don’t say it is because of your duty or my fixed timeline. You being here means a lot. It changed my life, and maybe it is destiny, I guess, but maybe it’s also…you know?” I cannot get past the first letter of what I am trying to say.

“I know, Aesena Varinas,” she agrees warmly, “I am very glad to have been in your company for the past month too. You’re brilliant beyond your imagination, and your imagination is endless.”

I blush at that, beaming.

I look up to the floating lights, then at the books that make the walls, then at the desk and its wings. What a month. I take out the pebble from my pocket, the blue shining pebble that is grey and un-shining now. I give it a good long stare, then I close my fingers around it. What a month indeed.

I will come back home soon, then I will make myself a hot cup of something, probably honeyhung chocolate, and I will ask if everyone in the manor could gather in the music room, and we will light the fireplace and just sit in each other’s silence, like that one time we did before. It was on my third night here, I think. I will start a diary too, one that refers to the time-travelling journal. There are many other things I want to do. But for now…

I turn back to where I am sure Miss Breezy is. I can sense her uneasiness. But I am calm, and now I think she is too.

“Year 331 first, right?”

“Right.”

“Right.”

I breathe in.

“See you in a week,” I say, the hesitation in my tone before now gone without a trace to be found.

“See you in five minutes,” she says, a cheer in her tone, her worries dissipated.

I turn to the gate. I step through it.
