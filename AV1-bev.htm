Bird's Eye View


On a drive from the small village of Maalos to the Le-Var Coastway, one would likely miss a windy lane branching off to curve around a hill. Though at first seeming a little shady, with the shadows of large leaves and overgrown bushes casting such a sinister appearance at the junction, when given an adventurous chance, it opens up to lush greeneries that reach out from tall lean trees, where under them are only well-mannered grass, flower bushes, and the occasional passing of a deer or a hedgehog. Going on further, it will again branch off to the city of Onleft and the Wholeone Highway Route Four that connected Soria with the rest of the continent, but neither is where this story is bringing us.

The lane gives a lengthy journey as it curves widely around groves and lakes. Far from its meeting with the highway while also far from its branching from the village road, it bends the widest around an enormous tree that has stood for a thousand years. Around the tree are smaller ones, all of which seem to be leaning towards it, and those that meet its trunk creep upwards, almost like they are merging into the width of the old entity. The locals call it ‘The Tree Of Trees’, or 'The Growmany Tree'. After this large curve, the lane then strikes across a river with the help of an ancient stone bridge. But we are not going there either. At least, not yet.

Directly opposite the big old tree and without a path leading to it, hidden from the lane by hedges and misbehaving ferns of a huge variety and at the same time flanked at the rear by shallow forests that deepens into the mysterious Hawkwoods, it is little wonder how The Round O’ Way Manor is rarely disturbed by passersby. On discovery, however, one would first smell the aroma of the daily bakes before being splashed by a burst of scents from flowers and herbs and honeyhangs and being further mesmerised by the melody of a morning piano, then one would marvel at the maroon stones that stack up here and there to become sheds and towers and mansion, altogether forming this great manor. Its three chimneys smoke no matter the time of day, and once each break of dawn and another each fall of dusk, the bell in one of its four towers rings.

The narration is about to end, for here comes our young protagonist. Through one of the many windows, a girl is seen stirring up a drink in a blue mug, adding ingredients by a pinch or a spoonful as she goes. It takes two full minutes for her to be satisfied with her work, then she places the cup with three others. She looks out the window to gleam at how the sunrays sheen the honeyhangs, and how the dewdrops are lit with the morning as they dangle from the leaves. Her shoulder-length black hair catches the sun as well, and her amber-gold eyes sparkle with a sudden wonder, as if she is aware of what the day will soon bring. She is not, of course.

It has been two weeks since her parents dropped her off in the middle of these greens and blues, with their work in the city so acalling. That meant sixteen whole days of nothing too exciting as she navigated through the adjustment of calling the place her new home, which can very possibly drive anyone mad. So the little Aesena Varinas took to herself the necessity of waking up early this very day to start making herself useful as an effort to spend her long pent-up energy, and to probably feel productive – and exhausted should this activity lead to another and then another – the next time she goes to bed. She has quite the positivity that many of us crave in our stories, but this is hers, and so it begins.
